GAMES 101 L19-Cameras

Focal Length (f)

  • The focal length represents the distance from the camera (eye) to the image plane (viewport) in world space.
  • A larger focal length means a more zoomed-in effect (narrow FoV).
  • A smaller focal length means a wider perspective (wide FoV).

Ray Tracing Interpretation

  • The focal length determines how far the virtual image plane (viewport) is placed in front of the camera.
  • In ray tracing, we assume a thin lens model, where the camera sits at (0,0,0), and the image plane is placed at z = -f.

Viewport Size (viewport_width, viewport_height)

  • The viewport is the imaginary 2D plane where the rays originate before traveling into the scene.
  • The size of the viewport depends on:
    • Aspect Ratio: (width / height), ensuring the image isn’t stretched.
    • Focal Length: Affects the scale of the viewport.
    • FoV: Determines how large the viewport appears relative to the scene.

Ray Tracing Interpretation

  • The viewport is usually defined in world coordinates at z = -f (for a forward-facing camera).
  • The camera shoots rays from its center of projection, passing through each pixel on the viewport.

Field of View (FoV)

  • The field of view (FoV) is the angular extent of the scene captured by the camera.

  • Defined as vertical FoV (θ), where:

    tan⁡(θ2)=viewport height/2focal length\tan(\frac{\theta}{2}) = \frac{\text{viewport height} / 2}{\text{focal length}}tan(2θ​)=focal lengthviewport height/2​

    or solving for viewport height:

    viewport height=2×f×tan⁡(θ2)\text{viewport height} = 2 \times f \times \tan(\frac{\theta}{2})viewport height=2×f×tan(2θ​)

  • A large FoV (e.g., 90°) results in a wide-angle lens effect, capturing more of the scene.

  • A small FoV (e.g., 30°) results in a zoomed-in effect.

Ray Tracing Interpretation

  • When setting up a camera in ray tracing, we often specify the FoV, then derive the viewport size accordingly.

Relationship Between Focal Length, Viewport, and FoV

  1. Focal Length and Viewport

    • The focal length (f) directly affects the size of the viewport.
    • Larger f → Smaller viewport (zoomed in).
    • Smaller f → Larger viewport (wider view).

  2. FoV and Viewport Size

    • Increasing FoV (wider angle) → Larger viewport.
    • Decreasing FoV (zoom-in) → Smaller viewport.

  3. FoV and Focal Length

    • They are inversely related:
      • Small FoV → Large focal length (zoom-in)
      • Large FoV → Small focal length (wide-angle)
